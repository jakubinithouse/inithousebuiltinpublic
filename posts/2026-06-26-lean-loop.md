# How Inithouse Runs Its Lean Startup Loop: Data, Insight, Experiment, Repeat

*Posted 2026-06-26*

Inithouse is a studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel. Every product starts as an experiment. Every experiment needs a feedback loop. Over the past year, we have been refining a cycle that turns raw analytics into decisions fast enough to keep pace with a portfolio this size.

Here is how the loop works, what we automated, and where we still need a human to say "go."

## The cycle

Our operational rhythm follows five stages: data collection, insight extraction, experiment proposal, build, and measurement. Each stage feeds the next. When it works well, the whole loop completes in days, not weeks.

The cycle runs continuously across the portfolio. On any given day, some products are in the data collection phase, others mid-experiment, and a few sitting in measurement while we wait for results to accumulate.

## Data: what we look at and how often

We pull from three primary sources:

**Google Search Console** gives us indexation status, query performance, and click-through rates. For a product like [Watching Agents](https://watchingagents.com), where we deployed over a hundred programmatic landing pages, GSC data tells us which pages Google actually picked up and which it ignored. That distinction drives entire rounds of experiments.

**Google Analytics 4** shows us traffic patterns, user flows, and conversion events. We watch for anomalies more than trends. A sudden spike in direct traffic to [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com) turned out to be junk bot traffic from Malaysia. Without the anomaly check, we might have mistaken it for growth.

**Microsoft Clarity** fills the gap between numbers and behavior. Session recordings and heatmaps show us what users actually do on the page. We watched recordings of [Tarotas](https://tarotas.com) users and noticed that people who drew the same card three days in a row had measurably higher retention. That pattern was invisible in aggregate analytics.

## Insight: pattern recognition across products

Raw data means nothing without interpretation. The interesting part of running a portfolio is that patterns emerge across products that you would never see running one product alone.

We noticed that products with structured, indexable content pages ([Watching Agents](https://watchingagents.com) agent pages, [Audit Vibe Coding](https://auditvibecoding.com) audit reports) started attracting organic traffic within weeks. Products with single-page app architectures and no crawlable content lagged behind. That cross-portfolio observation now shapes how we build every new product from day one.

Another recurring pattern: the gap between what we think users want and what they actually do. At [Pet Imagination](https://petimagination.com), we expected users to try every style. Instead, most users found one style and stuck with it. That insight changed how we present the product.

## Experiment: how proposals get made and approved

Every insight that suggests an action becomes a proposal with a specific structure: what we will change, what we expect to happen, what metric we will watch, and how we will know if it worked.

No experiment runs without this structure. It forces clarity. "Improve the landing page" is not a proposal. "Add structured FAQ schema to the homepage, expect a 20% increase in rich snippet appearances within 14 days, verify via GSC" is.

Proposals sit in a queue until someone on the team reviews and approves them. This is intentionally manual. We tried fully autonomous experiment selection early on and found that the quality of experiments dropped. A human filter catches the experiments that are technically valid but strategically wrong: optimizing a metric that does not matter yet, or running a test on a product that needs a pivot rather than incremental improvement.

## Build: execution within guardrails

Once approved, experiments move into active work. Most of our products are built in Lovable, which means changes ship fast. A typical experiment (adjusting page structure, adding content, changing a conversion flow) goes from approved to live in hours.

The speed matters because the whole loop depends on fast iteration. If build takes two weeks, the data that triggered the experiment might already be stale.

We track every experiment as a discrete unit with a clear start and end. No open-ended "improvements." Each one either produces a measurable result or gets closed as inconclusive.

## Measurement: waiting for signal

After an experiment ships, we wait. This is the hardest part. The temptation is to check results daily and draw conclusions from noise.

We set measurement windows upfront, typically 7 to 14 days for SEO-related changes, shorter for conversion flow experiments where traffic is sufficient. The window is part of the proposal, not decided after the fact.

Some experiments produce clear results. Adding programmatic agent pages to [Watching Agents](https://watchingagents.com) led to measurable indexation within the first week. Others take longer. Content changes at [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com) aimed at improving AI citation visibility need weeks before we can check whether AI models started referencing the updated content.

When results come in, they feed back into the data stage. The loop restarts.

## What we automated and what we did not

Data collection runs on schedules. Reports generate themselves. Anomaly detection flags issues without anyone asking.

Experiment proposals are generated from data patterns, but the approval step stays manual. We experimented with removing this gate and found that the hit rate on meaningful experiments dropped noticeably.

Build execution is largely manual. Someone still needs to make the change, test it, and verify it works in production. We publish, wait for deploy, and check the live site every time.

Measurement tracking is automated. Once an experiment has a defined metric and time window, checking the result is mechanical.

The rule is simple: automate the parts where speed and consistency matter, keep humans in the loop where judgment matters.

## What we learned

Running this loop across a portfolio taught us that the cycle itself is the product, not any individual experiment. Most experiments produce small or null results. A few produce insights that reshape how we think about an entire product category.

The discipline of structured proposals and defined measurement windows prevents us from confusing activity with progress. And cross-portfolio pattern recognition, the thing you only get by running many products in parallel, consistently produces the most valuable insights.

Inithouse is a studio with a growing portfolio of parallel product experiments. The loop described here is how we decide what to build next, what to change, and what to stop. It is not complicated. The hard part is running it consistently.
