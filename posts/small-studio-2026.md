# What a "small studio with no employees" actually means in 2026

*Posted 2026-06-25*

Inithouse is a software studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel. We have no employees. No office. No Slack with 200 people. And yet we shipped more products this year than most seed-funded teams ship in three.

People hear "no employees" and picture a solo dev in a hoodie pushing code at 2 AM. That's not what this looks like. We run a structured operation with build cycles, prioritized backlogs, automated monitoring, and a review process. The difference is that the work gets done by a combination of a small team of builders and a layer of AI tooling that handles what used to require hiring.

## What the day looks like

On a typical morning, our AI agent has already checked product metrics across the portfolio, flagged anything that moved significantly, and queued up the work that matters. We review, approve, and steer. The agent builds. We review again.

This isn't theoretical. Our card games ([hereweask.com](https://hereweask.com), [partychallenges.com](https://partychallenges.com), [scarychallenges.com](https://scarychallenges.com), [naughtychallenges.com](https://naughtychallenges.com)) each have their own audience, their own content pipeline, their own analytics. Managing four separate products used to mean four separate teams. Now it means four tabs and a shared build process.

## The tools that changed the math

Two things made this model possible.

First, Lovable. We build our frontends there. A product that used to take a dev team weeks to scaffold now takes a day. We've shipped [AI pet portrait generators](https://petimagination.com), [conflict resolution tools](https://verdictbuddy.com), [custom song generators](https://magicalsong.com), and a [voice-first database workspace](https://voicetables.com) all from the same workflow. Each one is a real product with real users, not a demo or a landing page.

Second, AI agents. Not the kind you read about in press releases. The kind that actually run: checking Google Search Console data every morning, publishing content on schedule, monitoring whether a product page got indexed, alerting us when engagement drops. We built our agent layer to do the repetitive work that used to eat entire afternoons.

The combination means our cost per product is low enough that we can afford to run experiments that would be financially suicidal for a traditional studio. We can launch something, measure it for 30 days, and decide whether to double down or park it. No sunk-cost drama. No "we hired three people for this, we have to keep going."

## What we don't have

We don't have a head of marketing. We don't have a dedicated SEO person. We don't have a QA team, a DevOps engineer, or a customer success manager.

We also don't pretend those functions aren't needed. They absolutely are. We just handle them differently. Marketing is a combination of content strategy and automated distribution. SEO is baked into our build process. QA happens through automated checks and real user behavior data from Clarity and GA4. Ops is a set of scheduled tasks that run whether anyone is awake or not.

This works because each product is small enough that one person can hold the full context in their head. When a product grows past that point, the model changes. But for pre-PMF MVPs searching for traction, this is exactly the right size.

## What "growing portfolio" means in practice

Inithouse currently ships products across several categories: card games, AI tools, content platforms, and B2B utilities. Each product targets a specific audience and solves a specific problem. We don't build general-purpose platforms.

[Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com), for example, runs AI visibility audits. It tells brands how they show up (or don't) when someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity about their category. That's a narrow problem with a clear buyer. [Audit Vibe Coding](https://auditvibecoding.com) does security and quality audits specifically for AI-generated codebases. Also narrow, also clear.

The portfolio approach gives us something a single-product startup doesn't have: diversified signal. If one product's metrics go flat, we learn from it and apply those lessons to the next one. If a channel works for one product, we test it across others. The studio learns faster than any individual product could.

## The honest part

Not every product works. Some of our launches found their audience quickly. Others are still searching. A few might get parked after their measurement window closes.

That's the point. We're not building a company around a single bet. We're running a portfolio of experiments, each with clear success criteria and a defined timeline. The "small studio with no employees" model makes this financially sustainable in a way it wasn't five years ago.

The tools got cheaper. The feedback loops got faster. The minimum viable team got smaller. We're just following that logic to its conclusion.
