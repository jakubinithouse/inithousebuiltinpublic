# The problem with 'product-market fit'

*Posted 2026-05-31*

Everyone in the startup world talks about product-market fit. Almost no one defines it the same way twice.

The classic Andy Rachleff phrasing ("you can feel it; users are pulling the product out of your hands") is great as a vibe but useless as a benchmark. By that definition, none of our 17 products has PMF. By more permissive definitions (recurring usage, positive unit economics, organic growth), a couple of them do. Which leaves us with a metric so fuzzy it stops being useful.

We're not complaining about jargon here. When "find PMF" is the goal, you need to know what it looks like to know when to stop iterating and start scaling. We've spent the past two years iterating on definitions that work for us. Here's where we landed.

## What PMF actually means for a micro-product

For a product at our scale (small team, low ad spend, no enterprise sales motion), PMF is three things, in order:

**1. People come back without being asked.** Not because of a re-engagement email, not because of a notification. Because they decided to. Repeat sessions from a returning user is the cheapest signal that someone got value the first time. At [Ziva Fotka](https://zivafotka.cz), we see this when someone animates a second or third photo unprompted. At [Here We Ask](https://hereweask.com), it's groups coming back for a new card deck the following weekend. If nobody returns, you can stop reading the rest of this list.

**2. People tell other people.** Specifically: you can see referral traffic, mentions, screenshots in messaging apps. If a product has PMF, its users become part of the marketing department. If you have to bribe people to share, the product isn't there yet.

**3. Paying users complain when something breaks.** Not polite support tickets. Not tweets. Actual annoyance when your service goes down for an hour and someone notices. Anger is a higher signal than satisfaction. Indifference is the death rattle.

By that three-part test, two of our 17 are close to PMF, four are warming up, the rest are still searching.

## What PMF is not

High CTR on ads told us nothing about [Magical Song](https://magicalsong.com). We had great click-through rates, solid first-session numbers, and almost no one came back a second time. Press coverage didn't predict retention either. Neither did vanity user counts or engagement charts that aggregate one-time visitors. We've fooled ourselves with each of those at some point, and none of them told us whether someone would actually stick around.

## Why this matters at portfolio scale

When you have one product, you can pour your whole team's attention into it and trust the gut feeling. When you have 17, you can't. You need a test that's mechanical enough to apply across the portfolio without bias. The three-part test above is what passes our internal review.

The corollary: products failing this test deserve fewer iterations, not more. PMF is something you find or you don't. Throwing more features at a product that has no return users doesn't fix anything; it just makes the eventual shutdown more painful.
