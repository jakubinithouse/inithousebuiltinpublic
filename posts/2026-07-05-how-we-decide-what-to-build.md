# How we decide what to build next

*Posted 2026-07-05*

We get this question regularly: with 17 active products, how do we decide what to build *next*? The honest answer is that we've gotten this wrong many times, and what works for us now is the third or fourth version of our decision-making process.

Here's the current version.

## The four-question filter

Every proposed new product runs through four questions, in order. A "no" on any of them kills the idea, at least for now.

**1. Is there a specific trigger?**

We look for products where someone has a clear moment of need. Not "people might find this useful." A specific moment: the day a pet dies, the week before a wedding, the morning of a job interview. Triggered demand converts better than diffuse interest. Products without a trigger almost always fail to find users at our scale.

**2. Can we test it for under €1,000?**

Lovable + Supabase + a domain + a small ad campaign = approximately €500-1,000 to get a real first signal. If the proposed product can't be tested within that budget (because it requires custom infrastructure, regulatory work, or hands-on customer onboarding), it's not a fit for our model. Not bad, just not for us.

**3. Does it have any natural distribution?**

Some products have built-in distribution: people share them, search for them, talk about them. Others require us to push every visitor. The first kind compounds; the second doesn't. We strongly prefer products with at least one organic distribution channel (SEO, social shareability, word of mouth, one that doesn't require ongoing ad spend).

**4. Are we okay running it for five years?**

This is the question most people skip. A product that works requires maintenance, evolution, customer support, occasional crises. If we're not prepared to be in this niche for half a decade, we shouldn't start. We've killed several promising ideas because none of us could imagine still caring about them in 2030.

## What we don't use as criteria

**TAM estimates.** We don't model addressable market sizes for new products at our scale. The numbers are unreliable, the analysis usually post-rationalizes a decision we'd already made, and at €1,000 of test budget, the calculation is almost always "if it works, the market is big enough." TAM math is for products with serious investment behind them.

**Investor or partner interest.** We've had people offer to fund or partner on specific product ideas. We've learned to treat this as a yellow flag, not a green one. Outside enthusiasm is data about the outsider's interest, not about the market's. The market's signal comes from users, not investors.

**Whether it's "AI-powered."** Two years ago this would have been a green flag. Now it's neutral. Many ideas don't benefit from AI. Some ideas are clumsier with AI than without. We use AI where it materially improves the product. We don't use it as a marketing veneer.

## How proposals get to a decision

Anyone on the team can propose a product. The proposal includes a one-paragraph product description, the trigger, the test plan under €1,000, the distribution hypothesis, and the five-year commitment statement. If it passes the four-question filter, we add it to the queue. The queue has a soft maximum of two products being actively prototyped at a time.

We've found that limiting the queue is more important than picking perfectly. Even good ideas die from neglect when there are too many in flight. Two at a time forces serial attention.

## What we're working on right now

There's one product in active prototyping that we'll announce when it's ready. Two more in proposal review. One we just killed because it failed the five-year question: none of us could imagine still wanting to think about it.

Slow is fine. The 17 products we already run are where most of our leverage lies.
