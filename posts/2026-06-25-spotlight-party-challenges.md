# Spotlight: Party Challenges. Building consumer games when you don't play consumer games

*Posted 2026-06-25*

[Party Challenges](https://partychallenges.com) is a collection of challenge-card games for groups. Think "Truth or Dare for adults, but actually fun." You pick a deck, gather some friends, and play through prompts designed to range from silly to slightly risky depending on the deck.

The product is one of Inithouse's better-performing consumer launches. It's also the product furthest from our team's natural taste, which is most of the lesson.

## How we got into a category we don't personally play

We weren't sitting around thinking "we should make a party game." The opening was data. We noticed search demand for very specific phrases like "questions to ask at a party," "drinking game ideas," and variations of these in multiple languages. Demand was high and growing, with poor product satisfaction among existing options. Most existing apps were either old, ugly, paywalled aggressively, or had decks that read like they were translated from a language that didn't exist.

A small, well-designed party game app with good decks and free access seemed like an obvious product. So we built it at Inithouse.

Our team, on the whole, does not throw parties. We don't play these games. We had to learn the genre.

## What we learned the hard way

**The first decks we wrote were too safe.** We wrote prompts the way technical people write prompts: clever, careful, low-risk. Players bounced. The data was clear: short sessions, no repeat use. We rewrote the decks with more edge, riskier prompts, more dares, more things that would actually generate stories, and engagement doubled.

**Card variety beats card cleverness.** A deck of 200 decent cards outperforms a deck of 100 great cards. We initially over-edited. Now we lean toward more cards, accepting that not every prompt will land. This applied across all four of our card games (Party Challenges, [Scary Challenges](https://scarychallenges.com), [Naughty Challenges](https://naughtychallenges.com), and [Here We Ask](https://hereweask.com) for conversation).

**The "host experience" matters more than the player experience.** One person chooses the deck and starts the session. That person is the gatekeeper. If the host doesn't have a good experience setting up the game, the players never see it. We rewrote onboarding around the host, not the player, and conversion improved.

**Localization here is brutal.** Humor doesn't translate. The Czech decks and the English decks have different prompts entirely. We've had to rewrite (not translate) every deck for every market. Worth doing, but expensive in time.

## What's working now

**Decks for specific occasions.** Birthday parties, bachelorette parties, road trips, dinner parties with new friends. Specific framings perform much better than generic decks. People search for the occasion, not the genre.

**Free with optional decks.** A free core experience with optional paid decks for specific occasions converts better than a fully paid app. The free experience gets people to bring it to their first party. The paid decks come later, when they've decided this is "their game."

**Word of mouth.** This is a category where if your friends use a product at a party you attend, you'll probably use it next time you host. We don't have to push for shareability. It's intrinsic to the use case.

## What's next

The category rewards continuous fresh content: new decks, new themes, new seasonal variants. We're building a content pipeline at Inithouse that lets the product evolve faster than we could write material ourselves, including a curator program for community-submitted decks (with editorial review). The infrastructure for that is in flight now.

For us, the lasting lesson is that "build outside your own taste" is sometimes the right call. The data was right. The market was real. We just had to be willing to learn a genre we wouldn't have come up with on our own.
