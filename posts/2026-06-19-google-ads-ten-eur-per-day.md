# Google Ads at €10 per day per product: what we learned running micro-budgets across a portfolio (Inithouse)

*Inithouse, a studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel. June 2026.*

Running Google Ads for a single product is well documented. Running it for a dozen products at once, each on a budget that would make most PPC managers laugh, is not. We do it anyway.

At Inithouse we run paid campaigns for a growing portfolio of niche products. Each product gets roughly €5 to €15 per day. That is less than a coffee and sandwich in Prague. Here is what that buys you, what it does not buy, and why we keep doing it.

## What €10 per day actually buys

Three things, and none of them is "scale."

**1. Baseline signal.** Before you spend anything, you have no idea whether your landing page converts, whether your copy resonates, or whether anyone clicks at all. Ten euros a day, running for two weeks, gives you enough data to separate "no signal" from "worth investigating." We run two variants per product (one leaner, one richer in creative assets) and compare conversion value against cost. Some products cross the breakeven line within the first month. Most do not. Both outcomes are useful.

**2. Funnel pressure test.** Organic traffic is slow and self-selecting. Paid traffic is fast and indiscriminate. It hits your weakest funnel step first. We found broken conversion tracking on two products, a registration flow that confused mobile users on another, and a pricing page nobody scrolled past on a fourth. All within the first week of spend. That is a €70 diagnostic.

**3. Keyword and audience data.** Google's search terms report is underrated for product positioning. We discovered that users searching for [Party Challenges](https://partychallenges.com) rarely use the word "party." They search for "drinking games," "dare games," and "group activities." That changed our SEO strategy, our landing page copy, and our app listing text. Same story at [Magical Song](https://magicalsong.com): "personalized song" converts, but "AI music generator" does not.

## What €10 per day does not buy

Reliable statistical significance. At this budget in Central European markets, you collect somewhere between 30 and 150 clicks per day per campaign, depending on the vertical. That is enough to spot patterns but not enough to run proper A/B tests. We treat the data directionally. If conversion value to cost ratio stays above 1.0 for four weeks, we consider scaling. Below 0.3 for four weeks, we pause and rethink.

It also does not buy competitive placements in expensive verticals. Some categories have CPCs above €2 in the Czech market. At €10 per day, you get five clicks. That is noise, not signal. We learned to avoid those verticals at this budget and focus on long-tail terms where CPCs sit under €0.50.

## Three things we stopped doing

**Chasing the lowest CPC.** Early on, we optimized every campaign for the cheapest possible click. That attracted bots, irrelevant traffic, and users who bounced in under three seconds. Now we let Smart Bidding find its own CPC within the daily cap and focus on conversion value instead.

**Following Google's recommendations.** Google's optimization score nudges you toward broad match keywords, expanded audience targeting, and higher budgets. We tried all three. Broad match diluted our search terms until the reports were useless. Expanded audiences sent traffic from markets we do not serve. Higher budgets just burned faster with no lift in return. We now sit at a low optimization score and ignore the alerts.

**Running Performance Max without guardrails.** PMax campaigns are Google's black box. They work, sometimes spectacularly, but you cannot see where your money goes. We run PMax in a "cheap" variant (minimal creative assets, low budget) as a canary. If the cheap variant shows signs of life, the product is worth more attention. If it flatlines for a month, we have our answer at minimal cost. We tested this pattern across products like [Pet Imagination](https://petimagination.com) and [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com), and the cheap/rich split consistently surfaces winners faster than scaling one campaign blindly.

## The CZ/SK advantage

Central European CPCs are 30 to 70 percent lower than Western European or US equivalents in most niches we operate in. A click that costs €1.20 in the US costs €0.30 to €0.50 in Czechia. For a studio running many products on micro-budgets, this is a structural advantage. We get three to four times the signal per euro spent. Products localized for the Czech and Slovak markets (like our card games and [Verdict Buddy](https://verdictbuddy.com)) reach statistical relevance faster than their English-language counterparts.

## When to scale, when to cut

We use a simple heuristic. After 30 days of spend, if conversion value divided by cost is above 1.0, the campaign pays for itself. We scale it by 20 to 30 percent and watch for another 30 days. If it stays above 1.0, we keep going. If it sits between 0.3 and 1.0, we keep the current budget and look for funnel fixes. Below 0.3 after 30 days, something structural is off and adding budget will not fix it.

Two products in our portfolio crossed the 2.0 threshold within the first quarter. Three more hover around 1.0. The rest sit below and serve as learning investments, not losses. Micro-budget ads exist to find out, quickly and cheaply, which products deserve real budget.

## The real output

We treat €10 per day per product as a measurement strategy, not an advertising one. It tells you where your product stands before you commit real money. It surfaces UX bugs, positioning problems, and conversion gaps that organic traffic alone would take months to reveal.

At Inithouse, a studio running parallel product experiments, we run micro-budget ads the way scientists run pilot studies: small, fast, and disposable. The expensive part was never the spend. It was always building something worth advertising.
