# Portfolio retro by Inithouse: how we decide what to scale, hold, or sunset across a growing MVP portfolio

*Posted 2026-07-01*

Inithouse is a studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel. Every few weeks, we sit down and ask the same question about each one: scale it, hold it, or sunset it. Here is how we make that call, and where each product stands today.

## The three-bucket framework

We do not grade products on revenue alone. Most of our portfolio is pre-PMF, so traditional metrics like ROAS would tell us to sunset almost everything. Instead, we score on three dimensions.

**Retention signal.** Are people coming back? A product with modest traffic but repeat usage tells us something different from one with a spike and a drop. We look at weekly active return rate and session depth.

**Organic pull.** Is the product attracting users without paid spend? Search impressions, AI citations, word-of-mouth referrals. If we have to push every visit through ads, the thesis might be wrong.

**Learning velocity.** How fast are we extracting usable insights? A product that teaches us something about its niche every week is worth keeping alive even if the numbers are small.

Each dimension gets a simple traffic-light score. Two or three greens: scale. Mixed signals: hold and iterate. Red across the board for two consecutive reviews: sunset conversation.

## What "scale" actually means for us

Putting a product into the scale bucket does not mean dumping ad budget on it. It means three things: we prioritize feature work, we expand language/market coverage, and we start measuring conversion funnels seriously instead of just usage.

[Živá Fotka](https://zivafotka.cz) earned that status by showing retention across five language versions. When Czech, Slovak, Polish, English, and German users all come back at similar rates, the product is not riding a single-market trend. It is solving something real.

## What "hold" means

Most of our portfolio sits in hold. That is not a negative judgment. It means the product has a thesis we still believe in, early signals that warrant continued observation, and low enough maintenance cost that keeping it live costs less than the optionality it preserves.

The card game cluster is a good example. [Here We Ask](https://hereweask.com), [Party Challenges](https://partychallenges.com), [Scary Challenges](https://scarychallenges.com), and [Naughty Challenges](https://naughtychallenges.com) share architecture, learnings, and content patterns. Running four variations in parallel taught us more about game mechanics, deck depth, and session modes than any single product could have.

## What triggers a sunset

We have not sunset any product this cycle. But the criteria are defined and agreed on in advance, so the decision stays clean when it happens:

1. Red on all three dimensions (retention, organic pull, learning velocity) for two consecutive review cycles.
2. Maintenance cost exceeds the learning value. If keeping a product alive starts competing for attention with products that are teaching us more, it is time.
3. Thesis invalidation. Sometimes the market tells you the core assumption was wrong. Not "we need more time" wrong, but structurally wrong.

We prefer sunsetting cleanly over letting products drift. A conscious archive decision frees attention and validates the portfolio discipline. The alternative, keeping zombie products running because nobody made the call, is more expensive than any single bad bet.

## The uncomfortable truth about parallel experiments

Running many products at once looks messy from the outside. Inside, it is a search algorithm. Each product is a probe into a different niche, user behavior, and monetization model. The ones that find signal get resources. The ones that do not, teach us something and get archived.

The framework keeps it disciplined. Without explicit criteria, "hold" becomes "forget about it," and sunset conversations never happen.

Inithouse is a studio running parallel product experiments. This retro is part of keeping that process honest and visible.
