# Spotlight: Voice Tables — what we're learning about voice as an interface

*Posted 2026-06-21*

[Voice Tables](https://voicetables.com) lets you fill spreadsheets by talking. You point at a column, say what should be there, and the rows fill in. Inventory, contact lists, simple databases — anything where the structure is regular and the entries are quick.

The product is interesting because the user base has turned out to be different from what we expected, and the lessons are useful for anyone thinking about voice as a primary interface.

## Who actually uses it

When we launched, we imagined the user as someone in a small business doing repetitive data entry. Some of those users exist and use Voice Tables that way. But the surprise was a second, larger group: hobbyists and people building personal systems. One user is tracking a multi-year book collection by talking to their phone in the car. Another runs a household inventory the same way. A third uses it for the data side of a personal research project they've been chipping away at for years.

These users were invisible to us pre-launch. They reached the product through search queries we hadn't optimized for, and they used it in ways the original UX didn't fully anticipate — in short, fragmented sessions, often hands-busy, often mid-task in something else.

## What we're learning

**Voice as a primary interface needs a different design surface.** The traditional spreadsheet view, optimized for typing, doesn't reward voice-first users. We're prototyping a voice-native view — bigger touch targets, less small text, fewer modal interactions, more confirmation feedback through audio. Early signal is positive.

**Adoption is slow because muscle memory is sticky.** Even users who would benefit from voice resist context-switching out of their existing spreadsheet flow. The people who do adopt are usually people whose flow is *already broken* — they're in a car, walking around inventory, hands occupied. That's a meaningful but specific population.

**The killer use case isn't "data entry."** It's "capture while in motion." Voice Tables is increasingly positioned as a capture tool that produces structured output, not as a spreadsheet alternative. The framing change has implications for marketing and product roadmap.

## Where it's heading

The next few releases focus on three things: voice-native UI for the people who use the product hands-free, mobile-first capture flows, and tighter integrations with the tools (calendar, inventory apps, personal finance) that the hobbyist users tend to already have.

This is the kind of product where the audience teaches you what to build. We came in with one hypothesis and the users have shown us a different — and arguably more interesting — one.

If you've ever wanted to capture structured data without sitting down at a keyboard, [try it](https://voicetables.com). It's still early, but the early users are using it more, not less, than we'd expected.
