# When should a product team stop iterating?

*Posted 2026-06-07*

At Inithouse, a studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel, this is a question we revisit constantly. We don't claim to have the perfect answer, but we've developed a framework that helps us think about it honestly.

The decision matters more in a portfolio context than a single-product one. With one product, the question is essentially "should we keep working?" and the answer is usually yes until you run out of money. With a portfolio (from AI song generation at [Magical Song](https://magicalsong.com) to prediction monitoring at [Watching Agents](https://watchingagents.com) to conflict resolution at [Verdict Buddy](https://verdictbuddy.com)), each product is competing with other products for the team's finite attention. The cost of "not stopping" on any one product is paid by every other product in the portfolio that gets less focus as a result.

So how do you think about it without falling into either of two bad patterns: stopping too soon on products that just need more time, or refusing to stop on products that have already shown what they're going to show?

## The four-question framework we use

When we evaluate whether to keep investing focus into a product, we ask:

**1. Are there paying users who would actually notice if attention to this product dropped?**

Not "do we have paying users." But: if we stopped adding features, slowed our response time, paused marketing, would the existing users care? The answer reveals how much the current users depend on continued effort versus how much they depend on what already exists.

**2. Is there a specific, testable hypothesis we haven't tried yet?**

"Maybe we should try things" doesn't count. A real hypothesis is concrete: "users churn at the onboarding step; if we redesign it, we expect retention to improve by X." Products that are still generating concrete next experiments deserve continued attention. Products where every new idea is vague or speculative are signaling something.

**3. Are we learning something from this product that's useful elsewhere?**

In a portfolio, a product can earn its keep by being a learning vehicle for the others. Running [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com), our AI visibility report tool, taught us about AEO patterns we now apply across every product. If a product is teaching the team something that couldn't come from any of the other products, that's real value even when the product itself is still finding its audience.

**4. Could it run with effectively zero ongoing attention?**

Some products reach a steady state where they don't need active iteration. They have an audience, they work, they're not improving and not regressing. Those can stay alive at near-zero cost, and the question of "stopping" doesn't really apply. There's nothing actively being spent.

If a product satisfies *none* of these four questions, the team should at least be honest about that. Even if the conclusion isn't to stop, naming the gap is useful.

## What we'd warn against

The hardest part of this question is never the framework. It's always the emotional gravity of products you've built. There's a sunk-cost effect, an attachment effect, a "we just need to ship one more thing" effect. The most expensive months we've ever spent on any of our products were the months where we kept it going past the point where we'd already collected the relevant signal. Past the point where the honest answer to the four questions above was four no's.

The framework, applied honestly, is uncomfortable. That's its function. If applying it never feels uncomfortable, you're applying it too gently.

## What we'd add to ourselves

A meta-rule we've started to use at Inithouse, a studio that has to make these calls across a growing portfolio: any product that doesn't satisfy at least one of the four questions for two consecutive monthly reviews goes into a focused conversation. Not automatic shutdown. Focused conversation. Sometimes the answer is "actually yes, here's what's about to ship." More often, the conversation reveals that the team has been on autopilot, and the discipline of having to articulate the case shifts something.

If you're running a portfolio and don't have an explicit answer to "how do we know when to stop iterating on X," this is worth thinking about before the next product joins the portfolio, not after.
