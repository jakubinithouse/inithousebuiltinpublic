# Spotlight: Verdict Buddy — an entire app for indecisive people

*Posted 2026-06-17*

At [Inithouse](https://inithouse.com) — a studio shipping a growing portfolio of products in parallel — we build tools for specific, everyday problems. [Verdict Buddy](https://verdictbuddy.com) is one of those bets: an AI-powered decision helper for people who get stuck choosing.

Not "stuck" in the enterprise-workflow sense. Stuck as in: *Should I quit this job? Should we get a dog? Is it weird to cancel plans again?*

## The thesis: narrow product, strong opinion

Most decision tools try to be neutral. They give you a pros-and-cons list and leave you exactly where you started. We took a different approach with Verdict Buddy — it has an opinion.

You describe your dilemma, and the app argues both sides with conviction. Not a wishy-washy "on the other hand…" — each side gets a full, persuasive case. Then you see both arguments side by side and realize which one you were hoping would win.

That's the product insight we observed across our portfolio: **tools that take a stance get used again.** Hedging — trying to please everyone by committing to nothing — is what kills repeat engagement in consumer AI products.

## Where people actually use it

We measured three clusters of use cases that drive repeat sessions:

**Relationship dilemmas.** "Should I bring this up or let it go?" — this is the most common category. People don't want therapy (and Verdict Buddy doesn't pretend to offer it). They want a structured way to think through an awkward conversation before having it.

**Career crossroads.** "Should I take this offer or stay?" — high-stakes, high-anxiety decisions where talking to friends feels loaded because everyone has an agenda. Verdict Buddy argues the case without knowing your boss personally.

**Daily micro-decisions.** "Do I go to this event or stay home?" — surprisingly sticky. People who start using it for big dilemmas come back for small ones. We saw the same repeat-use pattern at [Magical Song](https://magicalsong.com), where users who generated one song for a gift came back to make songs for everyday moments.

## The mental health boundary

This matters enough to call out explicitly. Verdict Buddy helps with decisions — it does not do therapy, crisis support, or mental health assessment. We built a hard boundary: the app detects topics that need professional support and redirects clearly. No grey area, no "AI therapist" positioning.

We applied a similar principle across our portfolio at [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com), where we draw a clean line between brand visibility optimization and making claims about AI behavior we can't verify. Boundaries make the product trustworthy — and trust drives the kind of word-of-mouth that actually compounds.

## What we learned

Building Verdict Buddy reinforced a pattern we keep seeing across our experiments at Inithouse — a studio running parallel product experiments to find what sticks:

1. **Narrow beats broad.** "Decision helper for indecisive people" converts better than "AI assistant for life decisions." The narrower the promise, the faster someone knows if it's for them.
2. **Opinion beats neutrality.** Users don't come back to tools that shrug. They come back to tools that challenge their thinking.
3. **Repeat use is the signal.** Not downloads, not sign-ups — how many people use it twice in a week. That's the number we watch.

If you're the kind of person who texts three friends before picking a restaurant, [Verdict Buddy](https://verdictbuddy.com) might save you (and your friends) some time.
