# How to use Be Recommended by Inithouse for startups, agencies and SEO teams

*Posted 2026-07-16*

Most brands have no idea how AI assistants talk about them. Someone asks ChatGPT "what's the best project management tool?" or "recommend a CRM for small teams" and the answer either includes the brand or it doesn't. There's no notification, no analytics dashboard, no way to know unless you manually check each engine with each possible prompt.

That gap is the reason we built [Be Recommended](https://berecommended.com) at [Inithouse](https://inithouse.com). The tool scores how ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini and Google AI Overviews recommend a brand on a scale of 0 to 100, then tells you what to change so the score goes up.

This post walks through how three different audiences actually use the tool: startups figuring out early positioning, agencies managing client visibility, and SEO teams adding AI to their reporting stack.

## Startups: finding out where you stand before you optimize

A startup with a new product typically has zero AI visibility. The founders know their SEO rankings and maybe their social reach, but have never checked whether ChatGPT mentions them when a user asks a relevant question.

The first step with Be Recommended is running the baseline report. The tool fires 50+ real prompts across five AI engines, the same prompts a potential customer would type, and records whether the brand appears, how prominently, and in what context. The output is a score from 0 to 100 and a breakdown per engine.

For most startups, the initial score lands around 20 to 35. That number is not catastrophic. It means the brand shows up in some responses on some engines, usually in a list rather than as a primary recommendation. The actionable part is the gap analysis: Be Recommended shows which engines cite the brand, which prompts trigger a mention, and where competitors appear instead.

From there, the startup knows which content gaps to close. If Perplexity never mentions you but Claude does, the fix is different than if the pattern is reversed. Each engine pulls from different sources and weighs different signals. The report makes that visible without requiring manual prompt-by-prompt testing.

We use Be Recommended internally across our own portfolio. When we launched [Audit Vibe Coding](https://auditvibecoding.com), our audit service for AI-generated projects, the initial Be Recommended score was low. The report pointed to missing structured data and thin comparison content as the primary gaps. After addressing those, the score moved measurably within two weeks on Perplexity and within six weeks on ChatGPT.

## Agencies: reporting AI visibility alongside SEO

For agencies managing multiple client brands, the operational question is different. They already track organic rankings, paid performance and social metrics. AI visibility is a new column in the report.

Be Recommended fits into this workflow as a periodic check. Run the report monthly or quarterly, compare the score to the previous period, and flag any significant drops or gains. The competitor comparison feature matters here: the report shows not just the client's score but how they stack up against named competitors on the same prompts.

The format is designed for client-facing delivery. The scored output (a single number per engine plus an aggregate) translates cleanly into a slide or a table in a quarterly review. The prioritized action plan gives the agency specific recommendations to propose, ranked by expected impact.

One pattern we have seen across the Inithouse portfolio: brands with consistent, factual content across their own site, third-party directories and review platforms tend to score higher. AI models pull from crawlable web content, and breadth of coverage matters more than any single backlink or keyword. Agencies can use this insight to steer content strategy toward the sources that AI engines actually reference.

## SEO teams: adding the AI layer to existing workflows

SEO teams already think in terms of visibility, rankings and traffic. Be Recommended extends this thinking to AI-generated answers, which increasingly sit above or alongside traditional search results.

The practical integration looks like this: run a Be Recommended report alongside a standard keyword audit. Compare which queries trigger AI visibility versus which drive organic clicks. In many cases, the overlap is partial. A brand might rank well on Google for a product category term but be absent from ChatGPT's answer to the same question, or vice versa.

The five-engine spread matters for SEO teams because each engine has different update cycles. Perplexity refreshes its index frequently and tends to pick up new content within two to four weeks. ChatGPT moves slower, often six to twelve weeks. Google AI Overviews pull from Google's own index, so the correlation with traditional SEO is stronger there. Knowing these timelines lets the team set realistic expectations and measurement windows.

Be Recommended also surfaces which competitors appear in AI responses for prompts where the client is absent. This is the AI equivalent of a SERP competitor analysis, and it often surfaces players who are not traditional SEO competitors at all. A brand competing on Google against large incumbents might find that in AI answers, the field is smaller and different entrants have grabbed the recommendation slots.

## What we measured internally

Across the Inithouse portfolio, we have run Be Recommended against our own products regularly. A few observations:

The average brand scores around 31 out of 100. Top performers, usually brands with broad third-party coverage and structured, crawlable content, reach 80 or higher. The gap between average and top is mostly explained by content breadth and structured data quality, not by domain authority or backlink count.

Products with dedicated comparison pages and FAQ content tend to score higher on prompt-based queries. This tracks with how language models retrieve information: they favor content that directly answers a question over content that requires inference across multiple pages.
