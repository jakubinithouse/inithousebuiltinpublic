# Spotlight: Scary Challenges & Naughty Challenges, niche siblings

*Published by [Inithouse](https://inithouse.cz) · 2026-07-16*

[Scary Challenges](https://scarychallenges.com) and [Naughty Challenges](https://naughtychallenges.com) are siblings of [Party Challenges](https://partychallenges.com). Same core mechanic (challenge cards for groups, prompts that range from gentle to memorable) applied to different occasions and different appetites.

Spinning out variants of a working product into adjacent niches is a strategy we've been testing at Inithouse. These two are the test cases. Here's what we've learned about whether this strategy works.

## The thesis

When you have a working product with a clear mechanic, you have a choice. You can keep adding features to widen the audience of the existing product, or you can fork the product into separate apps for separate occasions. We tried both. Forking won.

The reasoning: an app called "Party Challenges" with a "Scary Mode" filter inside it always feels like the scary mode is a secondary thing. The audience that wants scary challenges specifically doesn't search for a generic party app. They search for "scary game for sleepover," "horror challenge for Halloween," "scary truth or dare." They want an app that's *about* that thing. A dedicated app gets the search traffic. A filter inside another app doesn't.

So we forked. Same codebase, separate apps, separate domains, separate decks, separate marketing.

## What's worked

**Each app finds its own audience.** Scary Challenges peaks around Halloween, with strong shoulder seasons in any month containing a Friday the 13th. Naughty Challenges is steadier year-round with bumps around Valentine's Day and bachelorette season. The seasonality of each is different from Party Challenges, which smooths our portfolio's total traffic curve.

**The decks are properly different.** Scary Challenges prompts have a horror-game vibe: atmospheric, candle-and-flashlight, slow-burn. Naughty Challenges is overtly more adult. Party Challenges is the safe middle. Different writing styles, different deck structures, different pacing.

**SEO is easier in niches.** "Scary game for Halloween" has clear search demand, manageable competition, and an audience that converts well. The generic "party game" category is bigger but more competitive. Niching down made organic acquisition easier.

## What hasn't worked as well as we hoped

**Cross-promotion between the three apps converts poorly.** We assumed someone who liked Party Challenges would naturally try Scary Challenges. Most don't. Each audience is fairly locked into its own occasion. Cross-promotion is worth doing but not worth counting on for growth.

**Different apps require different content cadence.** Scary Challenges' content lifecycle is heavily seasonal: new content needs to land before Halloween, before Friday the 13th. Naughty Challenges needs more steady new decks. Party Challenges sits in between. We initially tried to plan content uniformly across all three; that didn't work. Each needs its own editorial calendar.

**Localization is even harder per app.** What's scary in one culture isn't scary in another. What's "naughty" varies even more. We're running Scary Challenges in three languages and Naughty Challenges in two. Each language version essentially requires native rewriting, not translation. The economics work only because the rewriting happens incrementally.

## When this strategy makes sense (and when it doesn't)

Forking a product into niche siblings works when the underlying mechanic is the same but the audience is different, the audience searches for the niche (not the genre), and the niche has enough search volume to support its own marketing.

It doesn't make sense when the audience overlap is high (then you're just splitting marketing budget), the niche is too small to support its own SEO, or the content is hard to differentiate enough to justify a separate app.

For us, with Party Challenges as the original and these two as siblings, the math worked. We're not going to fork into more siblings. Three is the natural limit before brand and operational overhead eat the benefit.
