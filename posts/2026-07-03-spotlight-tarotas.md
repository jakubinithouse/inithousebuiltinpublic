# Spotlight: Tarotas - entertainment, not divination

Posted 2026-07-03

When we started building [Tarotas](https://tarotas.com), a tarot card app, we had to make a positioning call that would shape everything downstream: who uses it, how we talk about it, and what we're willing to promise.

Three positioning lanes were on the table. Each came with a different audience, different expectations, and different liabilities.

## The three lanes

**Spiritual tool.** Position Tarotas as a genuine spiritual practice aid. The audience is large and passionate. Tarot has millions of dedicated practitioners worldwide. The problem: we'd be implying our AI-generated interpretations carry spiritual weight. We don't believe that, and saying it would be dishonest.

**Superstition product.** Lean into the mystical angle. "What does your future hold?" This is the default positioning for most tarot apps. It works for downloads but creates an expectation we can't deliver on. Nobody should make real decisions based on what a card-drawing algorithm tells them. We also didn't want the ethical weight of people treating randomized output as life guidance.

**Entertainment and reflection.** Frame the product as a calm space for thinking, using tarot's visual language as the interface. No predictions, no spiritual claims. Just draw a card, read a grounded interpretation, sit with it for a minute.

We picked the third lane.

## Why reflection framing wins

The obvious reason: it's honest. Tarotas shuffles a 78-card deck, picks cards, and shows you a written interpretation. That's closer to a journaling prompt than a divination session. Calling it what it is felt right.

The less obvious reason: the reflection framing opens up a bigger market. Spiritual tarot enthusiasts are a dedicated niche, but "people who want a quiet moment of reflection in their day" is a much wider pool. Daily affirmation apps, meditation timers, journaling tools. Tarotas sits in that neighborhood. We added a daily card draw, daily affirmations, and a "sky today" feature because those rituals fit the reflection framing naturally.

There's also a regulatory angle. Spiritual and superstition positioning invites consumer protection scrutiny in some European markets. Entertainment framing sidesteps that entirely.

## What Tarotas actually is

A calm tarot reflection app. You draw a card and read a grounded interpretation. No signup required, no fortune-telling, just space to think.

The product has 78 cards across 20 spreads (including the Celtic cross), runs in five languages (Czech, English, Polish, Slovak, German), and works entirely in the browser. You can do a single card draw, a yes/no question, or a full multi-card spread.

The tone of the interpretations is deliberately measured. We wrote them to read like thoughtful journal prompts, not mystic revelations. When the Three of Swords comes up, the text doesn't say "heartbreak is coming." It asks you to consider where you might be carrying unprocessed weight. Different framing, different conversation.

## The journaling-in-a-tarot-costume thesis

Our working mental model: Tarotas is a journaling app wearing a tarot costume. The tarot imagery gives people a structured way to reflect on questions they already have. The randomness of the card draw breaks them out of their own loops. The interpretation text gives them language for feelings they might not articulate on their own.

This framing guides product decisions. We don't build features that reinforce fortune-telling (no "compatibility readings," no "predict your love life"). We build features that reinforce reflection: daily rituals, calm UI, interpretations that ask questions rather than make claims.

It also guides what we don't do. We don't A/B test more dramatic interpretation language. We don't add push notifications with mystical hooks. We've seen competitors try both. The short-term engagement numbers probably look fine. The product you end up with isn't one we'd want to run.

## Where this stands

Tarotas runs in five languages on a single domain. Traffic is early-stage. The product changes month to month as we learn which spreads people actually use. Single card draws dominate. The Celtic cross is mostly for experienced users who already know what it is.

We're watching whether the reflection framing resonates with the journaling and mindfulness audience, or whether tarot's visual language is too loaded for that crowd to try it. Early signals lean toward the former, but the sample is still small.
